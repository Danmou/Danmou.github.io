The main goal for this thesis is to develop a solution for goal-oriented navigation in unexplored environments. This means we have a goal but we have no idea how to get there or what obstacles might be in the way. And I want to do this using only monocular RGB observations

And i want a solution with - As few assumptions about the agent, the environment or the task as possible - As little engineering or hand-crafted solutions as possible, as that will inevitably include assumptions - As little training data as possible, the hope being that the robot can one day train in real life instead of simulations - And for the same reason I want as little supervision or ground truth observations as possible So the dream is basically to one day have a hands-off solution where we just need to define the task in an intuitive way and then the robot can learn on its own, just like a human could

So the dream is basically to one day have a hands-off solution where we just need to define the task in an intuitive way and then the robot can learn on its own, just like a human could

A solution to this problem could be used for many tasks, for example search & rescue drones, or for other applications as long as you have some idea where your goal is but getting there might be non-trivial.

But, looking at the bigger picture, we can see this as a proxy task that tests how well our system can learn the skills needed for all navigation tasks

The specific task I will be using is the PointGoal task, as defined by Anderson et al. where you have a ground-based agent, you give it at each time step the coordinates of the goal relative to itself, and you say it's successful when it reaches the goal. Some of the benefits of using the PointGoal task are that it is easy to evaluate because you have this straightforward definition of success, and there already several papers evaluating various approaches on this task The downside is you need ground truth localization in order to provide the goals, which would make it difficult to train in real life.

I will be training and evaluating my model in simulation, specifically...

... specifically in the Habitat simulator made by Facebook. The environments come from large datasets of scanned interiors of buildings. I have configured it to have a one-dimensional continuous action space, where - you always move 25cm at each time step, and then you control how much to turn.

There are two basic approaches to this problem...

There's the hand-crafted one that where you...

There's the hand-crafted one where you split the problem into sub-problems and solve each individually. For PointGoal navigation in Habitat, this has been shown to work ok with RGBD input, but without depth it is much harder.

Then there's the RL approach, where you try to do the whole thing end-to-end, i.e. you find a general mapping from observations to actions. In order to do this we need a reward function, so we have a target to optimize for. I will get back to this in a moment. PPO, a popular RL method, has been shown to work ok, but you need a lot of training interactions.

Before I move on to my solution, I want to discuss the choice of reward function. This choice can make a very big difference, but it is often glossed over. The straight-forward choice for this task would be a sparse reward, where you give a reward for reaching the goal but otherwise nothing. This makes initial learning difficult because random actions are unlikely to lead to reward. You can help this problem by using curriculum learning, which is a method where you show only easy examples first and gradually make them more difficult But in most papers on this task, they just avoid the problem by using a dense reward. Here you add a reward for decreasing the geodesic distance to the goal at each time step, which is the length of the optimal path to the goal. Then there's the issue of collisions. Penalizing collisions encourages conservative behaviour, which can make it difficult to learn to navigate. Again, most papers choose to avoid the issue by allowing collisions and not penalizing them. But collisions in Habitat are friction-free so this leads to behaviours that wouldn't generalize to the real world. I will be using collision penalties in my experiments.

Model-based RL is a group of methods where you train a model to simulate the environment. This is motivated by the fact that humans are capable of thinking ahead and predicting the outcomes of their actions. These methods tend to be more data-efficient than model-free methods. I will use two related model-based methods called PlaNet and Dreamer.

I will use two related model-based methods called PlaNet and Dreamer. In PlaNet, observations are encoded to a smalled dimensional latent space for more efficient predictions. The model predicts both future observations and rewards, and then we can plan in an MPC-inspired receding-horizon fashion, where we optimize for most reward within some time horizon. Of course this means doing a lot of predictions at every step, so execution is much slower than model-free methods such as PPO. Limited planning horizon biases towards short-term benefit. Then there's dreamer, an extension of PlaNet that was just released two months ago. Instead of doing this slow MPC-style planning, we train a model-free policy using the learned model instead of the environment.

My main approach, which I have dubbed MerCur-Re, primarily consists of. - Adapting Dreamer to work for the PointGoal task. - Using Curriculum Learning during training as I proposed earlier. - And a little trick I call off-policy retraining, which I will explain in a moment. - I will evaluate it in comparison with PPO, PlaNet and non-learned baselines.

First let's look at the hand-crafted baselines. Random actions performs very badly. Goal follower goes straight towards the goal. These models were trained for more than 30M steps.

Here we see Dreamer without Curriculum Learning. Even in this case, it easily beats PPO in less than 800k steps. No problem with sparse reward.

When we add curriculum, it learns a lot faster initially but later it doesn't make much difference.

Here comes the off-policy retraining I mentioned. I discovered that if you simply reset all weights of the network to random initialization and train it again with the data you collected so far, it reaches a significantly better performance.
